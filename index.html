The Assault on Women’s Healthcare: A Sinister History of Reproductive Injustice Against Women of Color

How women of color continue to face reproductive healthcare injustice despite legal and medical progress

By Ariana Santiago

On September 11th, the Trump Administration ordered $10 million worth of birth control pills and other forms of contraceptives to be destroyed, and would no longer be purchased by the United States for low-income nations, falsely claiming that the pills induced abortions. This decision demonstrates the lack of control women, especially women of color, have over their own healthcare and reproductive healthcare needs that impact themselves and their families. Furthermore, on the same day, the U.S. Circuit of Appeals lifted a lower court’s preliminary injunction, allowing Trump to defund Planned Parenthood by blocking them from receiving reimbursements from Medicaid.

The percentage of Black and Hispanic women, who are covered by medicaid for births is 64% and 58% respectively. The destruction of birth control and medicaid restrictions impact women of color the most due to the fact, if they are low-income they rely on both, therefore their healthcare rights are under attack. Systematically they are under not just a pay-gap, but an insurance gap. Without medicaid, access to maternal and reproductive healthcare is limited.

In accordance to the Trump Administration’s advancement of Project 2025, they aim to eliminate coverage for birth control from the Affordable Care Act, encourage legal protection for employers and pharmacists who refuse to provide contraception and reproductive health care by citing personal religious and moral exemptions, “pass a law requiring equal (or greater) benefits for pro-life support for mothers and clarifying abortion exclusions,” suggesting that individuals who seek abortion care or contraception, through their employee benefits, may find themselves with limited or no access to benefits for reproductive health care.

Let's see how women of color have consistently faced reproductive healthcare injustice.

19th Century:

  • J. Marion Sims, the “Father of Gynecology,” surgically experimented on enslaved African women without anesthesia between 1845 to 1849.

20th Century:

  • In 1976 the Hyde Amendment, a discriminatory policy, barred federal Medicaid funds from being used to cover abortions which greatly impacted Black and Hispanic women.
  • In the 1970s, it was discovered that under the Eugenics Comission of North Carolina, 7,686 sterilizations had been carried out since 1933. With 5,000 of those that were sterilized had been Black.
  • By 1976, 24% of Indian women had been sterilized.
  • By the 1960’s, there was a 20% decline in population growth in Puerto Rico, and by the 1970’s, over 35% of Puerto Rican women had been surgically sterilized.

21st Century:

  • In 2022, Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization overturned Roe v. Wade, which eliminated the constitutional right to an abortion, thereby leaving individual states to limit or ban the practice.
  • In his first week in office , Trump signed an Executive Order committing to enforcing the Hyde Amendment, which then revokes two Executive Orders that Biden signed during his presidency—one that encouraged the government to widen and protect reproductive healthcare access, and another that categorized abortion as healthcare.
  • The Trump Administration announced that the U.S. was rejoining the Geneva Consensus Declaration, a non-binding pact the U.S. had joined toward the end of Trump’s first term that promotes anti-abortion policies
